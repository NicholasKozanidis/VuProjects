Question 3

Acquaint yourself with principles of color contrast, e.g. through the Color Contrast Check (Links to an external site.)Links to an external site.. Test three color combinations and write a short report of the results and how these should be interpreted.

Color contrast is an accessibility issue. Colors that are not contrasting enough can cause problems for visually impaired people to tell the content apart from the background. Colors should have a significant enough difference in both brightness and color to be accessible. Comparing a white background and a white text gives clearly the best results, as black and white are significantly different in both brightness and in hue. Dark blue and bright green result in a significant enough brightness contrast so that it would look good on a black and white screen, but fall short on the color contrast, so that it might cause issues for a visually impaired person. Bright green and bright pink together result in a significant color difference, but a very low brightness difference. So someone who has trouble telling colors apart should be able to read it, but it would not look good on a black and white monitor.

Question 5

Summarize the key multilingual issues that W3C is addressing within the "Internationalization" activity.

The multilingual issues in internationalization are all about how you write and read differently in different languages. The first issue is different characters. For instance greek, russian, many asian languages etc. use different alphabet than the latin alphabet, so there needs to be support for multiple character sets. The second issue is that in some languages you read and write in a different direction than in english, like right to left, or vertically. International apps and websites need to be built in such a way that if you mix languages written in different ways, they do not interfere with each other. There is also the issue that some languages have significantly different sentence structure, text needs to be made such that if it is translated, the length and structure of the text does not change dramatically. Lastly, culture-specific symbols can cause an issue. Symbols in one culture can mean something completely different in another culture, so when translating text, one must also take care to translate symbols.

Question 2

a. How do you assess the quality of the TF-based search. What can you say about precision and recall?

The only way to assess the quality of any search, including TF-based, is human assessment, or asking the user's feedback on results. Precision means how many of the received results were correct results, and recall means how many of the totally available correct results were found. For purely TF based search, the recall should be high, but presicion may be low as the term searched might be a common one, and might be found all over.

b.Computing the TFIDF matrix takes quite some time. Why is that, do you think?

That is because the entire text's worth of data needs to be processed multiple times. Calculating only the TF matrix needs just one run through as the words are counted, but for TFIDF, the IDF needs to be calculated for each word after the word count, and after that the total value needs to be applied to each word in each chapter based on the previous calculation and the total amount of words.

c. Compare the TF-based search with the TFIDF-based search. What has changed? How do you compare the quality of the searches. What can you say about precision and recall?

Both searches give you different results. TF gives you a raw word count of a spesific word, while TFIDF gives you a score that tells you how informative the word is in any given chapter. With a TF based search, searching for instance a spesific phrase, you would get high recall as you would find all the parts containing any of the words, including the correct phrases, but the presicion would be, depending on the words in question, high to very low. For a similar query using TFIDF, the presicion would be high, as any non-important instances of the words would be discarded immediately, but recall might also be lower, if some instances of the words are deemed unimportant.

d. Alice in Wonderland only has 11 chapters, what can you say about the precision at k where k=5 for both types of searches. That is, what if you would only look at the 5 highest ranked results, and discard the other results as irrelevant.

For TF, the precision and recall would both likely drop, since the search would only return the highest densities of certain words, but not necessarily together. TFIDF would have likely high precision, but not necessarily a high recall, since the top 5 results would likely be correct since they are selected based on the most relevant information, but it might drop out a lot of correct results beyond the top 5.
